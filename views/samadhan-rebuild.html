1. Why this matters

SSEI signed Samadhan to rebuild the website. Samadhan are builders, not writers. Before they can ship a single page, they need a finished page-by-page content brief: what the page says, in what order, with what SEO targets, linking where. That brief did not exist on Monday morning.

The choice was either (a) Samadhan writes guesswork content because no one gave them a brief, and SSEI catches errors in QA at the end — costing 4 to 6 weeks of rework — or (b) Growth & Ops gives them a complete, source-verified, SEO-optimised brief upfront so they build it right the first time.

This work is option (b). It is the spine the new website is being built on.

4. The science behind the content

Vidyut asked how SSEI's content gets written. The honest answer is it is not vibes — it is a repeatable 8-step process. Every page in the master document went through the same pipeline. Here it is, in order.

Step 1

Sitemap development

Start by listing every page that will exist on the website. One row per page, with eight columns: page ID, URL, tier (P0 launch-critical, P1 launch-nice, P2 post-launch), audience (visitor / student / admin), template type, indexability (yes / no for SEO), mockup file reference, source content document.

For this rebuild: 49 mockups from Samadhan, condensed and re-prioritised to 36 indexable pages plus a handful of templates.

Why this comes first. You cannot write what you do not know exists. Locking the page list before writing kills scope creep and tells every contributor exactly which pages they need to provide source content for.
Step 2

Skeleton framework (slot A–R)

Every page is built from the same alphabetical slot grid. Course pages use slots A (Breadcrumb), B (H1 + Badge row), C (Meta row), D (Tabs), E (Overview lead paragraph + What’s Inside list), F (Curriculum), G (Faculty), H (Mocks), I (FAQs 5x), J (Toppers), K (Related Courses 3x), L–R (Sticky Purchase Card with pricing, validity extension, CTA).

Public marketing pages use a variant. Engagement pages a third. The slot pattern stays consistent within each page type.

Why this matters. Consistency. The reviewer does not have to re-learn each page’s layout. Chirag and Shubhankar both fill the same skeleton, so the master assembly stays clean. Build once, reuse 36 times.
Step 3

SEO research per indexable page

Search engines rank pages, not websites. So every one of the 35 indexable pages got its own SEO research pass:

  • Primary keyword (1 high-intent target, e.g. “CFA Level 1 coaching India”)
  • Secondary keywords (3 to 5 variants, e.g. “CFA L1 Sanjay Saraf”, “CFA Level 1 online classes”)
  • Long-tail / question keywords (2 to 3 for FAQ-stage searchers)
  • Meta title (under 60 characters, includes primary keyword)
  • Meta description (under 155 characters, CTR-optimised, action-oriented)
  • H1 (under 70 characters, exactly one per page)
  • Open Graph title and description (for LinkedIn / WhatsApp shares)
  • Schema.org type (Course / Organization / Person / FAQPage / BreadcrumbList) with structured fields
  • Internal-link targets (every page links to 3+ related pages)

Sources for the keyword research: research_findings.md (29K of keyword clusters, competitor SERP intel, search-intent breakdowns), live ssei.co.in product pages, and Google’s autocomplete / People Also Ask scraping.

Why this matters. A course detail page that ranks first for “CFA Level 1 coaching India” is worth more than the entire homepage combined. Per-page SEO is how you compound long-term organic traffic.
Step 4

Annotated mockups (visual cross-reference)

For the four most-reviewed pages (Homepage, About, Mocks listing, Mock detail), the HTML mockups were annotated with blue floating slot badges — TL-A, TL-B, MD-A, MD-B etc. — so a reviewer can see “this paragraph in the brief fills slot E on the mockup” at a single glance.

Why this matters. When the content writer and the designer are different people (and they always are), the annotated mockup is the bridge. No more “which slot does this go into?” in QA.
Step 5

First-pass team verification (source intake)

Before any content gets written, the source-of-truth docs are collected and verified:

  • Vidyut signs off on the master pricing sheet
  • Chirag delivers 15 course content docs (CFA, FRM, CA, Analyst Stack, Prudentia)
  • Shubhankar delivers 7 ACCA docs
  • Faculty Affairs returns the fact-check responses
  • Sales delivers the FAQ pool

These get extracted (DOCX → markdown via pandoc, PDF → text via pdftotext, XLSX → CSV via libreoffice, PPTX → XML regex) into a structured _extracted/ folder. Every page brief later cites which source file it used.

Why this matters. No fact in the master document is “made up.” Every number, hour count, price, faculty name traces back to a named team contributor. If something is wrong, you know exactly who provided it and where to send the fix request.
Step 6

Detailed content writing

36 individual page briefs written into per-page markdown files. Each file has SEO frontmatter (title, meta, OG, schema) at the top, then slot-by-slot copy in mockup order. Facts come only from the verified Step 5 sources. SSEI’s voice rules apply throughout:

  • No flowery / consulting language
  • No Instagram-style sentence fragments
  • Web-content register (authoritative, clear, never catchy)
  • Specific numbers always (10 lakh, 30 years, four credentials) not approximations
  • Product facts unchanged from source; phrasing SEO-polished
Why this matters. Voice drift across 36 pages is the most common failure mode. Locking the voice rules upfront and self-policing through writing keeps the master document feeling like one document, not 36 stitched-together drafts.
Step 7

Second-pass team verification (cross-check)

After the pages are written, a second verification loop catches any factual errors introduced through phrasing. Course detail pages go back to Chirag (or Shubhankar for ACCA) for a spot-check. Pricing tables go back to Vidyut. Faculty bios go back to Faculty Affairs.

This is also where decisions like “remove Karan Aggarwal from all faculty references” (he left SSEI) and “strip FRM Part 2 from combos” (out of scope per directive) get applied across the entire document.

Why this matters. Even a careful writer occasionally introduces an error through paraphrase. The second-pass catches it before vendor handover, when fixing it costs 10x more.
Step 8

Final SEO optimisation + 3-pass recheck

One last sweep across the master document with three independent passes:

  1. Structural pass. Every indexable page has complete SEO frontmatter. No missing meta description. No missing schema. Heading hierarchy logical (H1 once, H2 sections, H3 subsections).
  2. Voice pass. Em-dash sweep (none in original prose). Instagram-fragment sweep (none). Hyped-claim sweep (every claim grounded in a source).
  3. Cross-reference pass. Internal links resolve to existing pages. Pricing matches between course detail and master sheet. Faculty names spelled consistently. Combos link to existing combo URLs. Stale references (e.g. removed faculty) stripped.
Why this matters. This is the difference between a content document and a publishable spec. Samadhan can build directly from the master doc without coming back with “what about X” questions, because X is already cross-referenced.

That is the content science. 8 steps, 5 working days, repeated for every page. The output is not just words on a wall — it is a vendor-ready spec a third party can build from without further input.
